Adidas in Naupan: when the story
outruns the facts.

Company Adidas / Someone Somewhere

Country México · Puebla

Period 2026

Context

A feel-good story in a volatile landscape.

For the 2026 World Cup, Adidas partnered with Mexican social enterprise Someone Somewhere to produce a limited hand-embroidered jersey. The canvas: 150 Nahua women from Naupan, a mountain community in Sierra Norte de Puebla. The product: 2,026 numbered pieces retailing at up to $285 USD each, each tagged with a QR code linking to the artisan who made it. Two women were flown to Adidas headquarters in Germany to have their work archived. Others walked onto the pitch during a pre-tournament friendly.

The project had everything a brand story needs — craft, identity, purpose, scarcity. It sold out fast.

The challenge

A viral accusation with a 48-hour head start.

Before any corporate response existed, cultural promoter Luz Valdez published a video to her 1.3 million combined followers on TikTok and Instagram. Her claim: the women were paid 36 pesos — roughly $2 USD — an hour to embroider jerseys that sold for thousands. No social security. Non-traditional techniques imposed by quality control in Hong Kong. A public cultural center converted into a private maquila. Confidentiality agreements that silenced the workers.

The video reached 83,000 likes before the companies said a word. News outlets picked it up. Politicians responded. The narrative was set.

The communications problem was not only the accusation. It was the asymmetry: a verified, emotionally charged story on one side, and silence on the other.

The response

Two very different decisions under pressure.

Someone Somewhere issued a formal statement on May 27, 2026. It argued the artisan collective operated autonomously, that wages exceeded minimum wage, that the Casa de Cultura was chosen by the collective itself for accessibility, and that a private health insurance plan supplemented the community's existing IMSS Bienestar coverage.

The statement also confirmed, without appearing to intend it, three of the central accusations: the artisans lacked formal IMSS employer registration, traditional embroidery techniques had been replaced by commercial stitches to meet Adidas quality standards, and a public building had been used as a private workshop. The attempt at transparency became partial confirmation.

Adidas chose a different path: minimal exposure. A two-line statement citing "adherence to global standards" and "strengthened working conditions." No press conference. No on-record executive. No field visit.

Then the New York Times went to Naupan.

"If all those people making those comments took the time to come and talk to us, they'd realize we're not being exploited."— Betty Alonso, artisan, Naupan · The New York Times, June 2026

Analysis

The gap between reach and rigor.

The New York Times sent a reporter to Naupan. He spoke with more than two dozen women, reviewed pay stubs, cross-checked numbers against living wage benchmarks for rural Mexico, and documented both what held up and what did not. His conclusion: the artisans contradicted the central exploitation narrative, wages were above 36 pesos per hour, and the work was valued by those who did it. He also documented real frictions — inconsistent income, non-traditional techniques, the supplier rather than employee classification — without conflating imperfection with abuse.

Valdez's sources were artisans who had declined to participate and anonymous former employees. That is a methodologically significant detail that none of her three viral videos addressed.

The case illustrates a structural tension in modern communications: social media rewards speed and moral clarity; journalism rewards verification and complexity. When a story is emotionally legible — luxury brand, indigenous women, poverty wages — the viral version travels faster than the nuanced one, and arrives first to the people who make decisions.

What the companies lacked was not a clean operation to defend. What they lacked was a communications architecture built before the crisis: public documentation of wages, methodology, and community consent that could be pointed to immediately. Instead, the first detailed defense of the project came from a third party — a newspaper — not from the brands themselves.

The confidentiality agreements signed by the artisans, legitimate in commercial and legal terms, became the most damaging element in the court of public opinion. Not because they implied wrongdoing, but because they created a silence where a response should have been.

Result

Pending — and that is part of the lesson.

The limited jerseys sold out. The brand collaboration continues: Someone Somewhere and Adidas announced a second collection for the 2026 World Cup cycle, and promotional materials maintained the same cultural narrative through June 2026.

Reputationally, the outcome is unresolved. Someone Somewhere's communication deficit — a statement that confirmed what it sought to deny — left the controversy alive in Mexican media. Adidas absorbed the story without visible long-term damage at the global level, protected in part by the scale differential between Mexican activist media and its core consumer markets.

The New York Times piece did not exonerate the operation. It complicated a narrative that had been made artificially simple. Whether that complexity reaches the audiences that most damaged the brands remains an open question.
